## History & Heritage

Cherry blossoms have been celebrated in Japan for over a thousand years. The tradition of hanami — "flower viewing" — dates to the Nara period (710–794 CE), when Japanese aristocrats would gather beneath the blossoms to compose poetry and celebrate the fleeting beauty of life.

The iconic association between cherry blossoms and Japanese culture deepened during the Heian period, when the blossom replaced the Chinese plum blossom as the floral symbol of the emperor's court. Poets began using Sakura as the default metaphor for beauty in all its forms.

During the Edo period, hanami became accessible to commoners, transforming from an aristocratic pastime into a beloved national tradition celebrated by all social classes.

## Cultural Significance

The ephemeral bloom — lasting only one to two weeks — represents mono no aware, the Japanese philosophical concept of the pathos of things and the poignant beauty of impermanence. Each fallen petal is a reminder that life is precious precisely because it is brief.

## Field Facts

- A single tree can produce up to 15 flowers per cluster
- Cherry blossoms are edible — salted and preserved in Japan
- Washington D.C. received 3,000 trees as a gift from Tokyo in 1912
- The world's oldest cherry tree is over 2,000 years old in Yamanashi, Japan
