## History & Heritage

Named for its resemblance to a tropical bird in flight, Strelitzia reginae was first described scientifically by Sir Joseph Banks in 1788, following its introduction to Britain from the Cape Province of South Africa. It was named in honor of Queen Charlotte of Mecklenburg-Strelitz, consort of King George III and patron of Kew Gardens.

In its native South Africa, the plant grows wild in coastal areas and along riverbanks. The specific epithet reginae means "of the queen" in Latin — a nod to its imperial bearing.

The flower has evolved an extraordinary pollination mechanism. The blue petal lobes form a specialized platform. When sunbirds land to drink nectar, their feet trigger a hidden anther, which deposits pollen directly on the bird's feet — ensuring cross-pollination with military precision.

## Cultural Significance

The Bird of Paradise is the official flower of the City of Los Angeles, representing the city's diversity, vibrancy, and unshakeable optimism. It has become a global symbol of paradise and joy.

## Field Facts

- Plants can live for over 50 years
- Seeds have a bright orange tuft to attract birds for dispersal
- The plant is mildly toxic to cats and dogs
- It can take up to 4 years for a seedling to first bloom
