## What is a Forest Ecosystem?

A forest ecosystem is one of the most complex and interconnected systems on Earth. It encompasses not just the trees, but the soil, microorganisms, fungi, animals, water systems, and atmospheric interactions that together form a living, breathing whole.

Forests cover approximately 31% of the Earth's total land surface — roughly 4.06 billion hectares — and are home to more than 80% of the world's terrestrial biodiversity. They are the lungs of our planet, absorbing carbon dioxide and releasing the oxygen that all animal life depends upon.

## The Wood Wide Web

Beneath the forest floor lies one of nature's most astonishing networks: the mycorrhizal network. Fungi extend their thread-like mycelium through the soil, connecting the roots of different trees in a vast underground communication system.

Through this "Wood Wide Web," trees share nutrients, water, and even chemical warning signals. A mother tree — the oldest, largest tree in a grove — can identify its own seedlings among hundreds and preferentially route more carbon and nutrients to its offspring. Forest intelligence is not metaphor. It is measurable, documented fact.

## Field Facts

- A single oak tree can support over 500 different species
- Trees can communicate distress signals through chemical compounds
- The Amazon rainforest creates its own rainfall through transpiration
