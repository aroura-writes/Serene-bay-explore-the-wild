## Deserts: Not Empty, Alive

The word "desert" conjures emptiness, but deserts are among the most ecologically fascinating environments on Earth. Covering approximately one-third of Earth's land surface, deserts are defined not by heat but by aridity — receiving less than 250mm of rainfall per year.

Life in deserts has evolved solutions of extraordinary ingenuity. The Namib Desert fog beetle collects drinking water from coastal fog on its textured back — a mechanism now being studied by engineers designing atmospheric water collectors. The Saharan silver ant can sprint at 855 millimeters per second, the fastest-known land speed of any ant species, to minimize exposure time on scorching sand.

## The Desert at Night

The desert reveals its truest self after dark. As temperatures plummet by 20–30°C within hours of sunset, the desert comes alive with activity suppressed by day. Sidewinder rattlesnakes emerge to hunt. Desert jerboas bound across the cooled sand. Scorpions — invisible by day — glow an ethereal blue-green under ultraviolet light.

Desert night skies, free from humidity and light pollution, offer some of the clearest astronomical viewing on Earth. The Atacama Desert in Chile hosts more major telescopes than anywhere else in the world, gazing into the universe from one of Earth's most otherworldly landscapes.

## Field Facts

- The Atacama Desert has areas that have never recorded measurable rainfall
- Desert plants can remain dormant for decades, then bloom within 24 hours of rain
- The Sahara was once a lush, green savanna just 11,000 years ago
